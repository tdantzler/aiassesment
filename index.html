Area 01 — Inquiry Capture

How many potential customers try to reach your business — and how many actually connect?

Every inquiry that goes unanswered is a potential customer who will likely call a competitor next. This area looks at each channel where customers try to reach you, and measures the gap between inquiries that come in and inquiries that actually get a real response.

What we're measuring

The inquiry-capture rate across your channels

For each channel you use — phone, web forms, social DMs, etc. — we estimate four things: how much volume comes in, what percentage goes unanswered or delayed past the point of usefulness, what percentage of those inquiries would have become customers, and what a customer is worth to you.

The calculation is simple: volume × miss rate × conversion rate × customer value = annual opportunity cost.

Industry benchmark: 20–45% of inbound inquiries go unanswered in small businesses, depending on channel.

Area 02 — Response Speed

When a lead reaches out, how fast do they hear back?

Speed of first response is one of the most underrated variables in sales. Leads don't wait — they fill out two or three forms, then go with whoever calls first.

What we're measuring

The conversion lift from faster first contact

The difference between your current close rate and the close rate you'd hit if every lead got an instant first response. The gap between those two numbers, multiplied by your lead volume and customer value, is the opportunity.

Research benchmark: A lead contacted within 5 minutes is up to 21× more likely to convert than one contacted after 30 minutes. (Harvard Business Review / Lead Response Management Study.)

Area 03 — Manual Workload

How many hours each week are spent on repetitive work that automation could handle?

Repetitive, rule-based work is the clearest automation target. Not creative work, not judgment calls — just the tasks that look the same on Monday as they did last Friday.

What we're measuring

Labor cost tied up in automatable work

Hours per week spent on repetitive work, multiplied by the loaded hourly cost of whoever does it, multiplied by what percentage could reasonably be automated.

Common examples: pulling the same report weekly, copying data between systems, sending similar emails, generating invoices from completed work, updating multiple calendars. If someone does it the same way every time, it's a candidate. 60–80% automatable is a realistic range for most of these tasks.

Area 04 — System Friction

How much time is lost moving the same information between systems that should talk to each other?

When a new customer is entered into the CRM, then retyped into the invoicing tool, then added to the email marketing platform, then scheduled in the calendar — that's four hops for one customer. This cost rarely shows up on a P&L, but it's real.

What we're measuring

Labor lost to between-system handoffs

Count the people doing data-movement work, the number of copy-paste or retype events per person per day, and the average time per event (including error correction). Multiply by a 250-day work year and the loaded hourly cost.

Typical small business uses 7–12 tools daily. Teams of 4–6 typically lose 200–500 hours per year to this friction without realizing it.

Area 05 — Follow-up Gap

What happens to the leads that don't close on the first contact?

Most sales require multiple touches, but most businesses stop after the first one. The leads that show interest but don't buy right away are usually the highest-value ones to recover — you've already paid to get them.

What we're measuring

Revenue recoverable from leads that went cold

Of leads who don't close on the first interaction, how many get no follow-up at all? Of those abandoned leads, what portion could be recovered through consistent, automated nurture — a sequence of thoughtful touches over time, rather than a single reminder?

Research benchmark: 80% of sales require 5+ follow-ups, but 44% of salespeople give up after one. Proper nurture can recover 8–15% of leads that otherwise go cold.
